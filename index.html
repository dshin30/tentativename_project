NYC PM2.5 & Traffic Volume Analysis (2016–2025)

A data-driven story about air quality, traffic behavior, and wildfire impacts.

New York City Overview

New York City is one of the most densely populated urban regions in the world. With millions of residents, five major boroughs, and heavy daily traffic, air quality is a critical public health priority.

Figure 1. The five boroughs of New York City.

What is PM2.5?

PM2.5 refers to particulate matter smaller than 2.5 micrometers—small enough to travel deep into the lungs and enter the bloodstream. These particles come from vehicle emissions, industrial activity, building fuel combustion, and wildfire smoke.

Figure 2-b. Cartoon representation of PM2.5 as harmful particles.

Traffic Conditions in NYC

Traffic congestion is one of NYC’s most recognizable features. Vehicle emissions contribute significantly to PM2.5 levels—especially during peak commuting hours.

Figure 3. Heavy traffic commonly observed in Manhattan.

Wildfire Smoke Impact

In 2023, massive wildfires in Canada pushed smoke across the East Coast. NYC recorded some of the worst PM2.5 levels in modern history.

PM2.5 & Traffic Visualizations

Figure 5. Annual PM2.5 averages (2016–2025).

This heatmap shows how yearly average PM2.5 changed from 2016 to 2025 in each NYC borough. While normal fluctuations appear every year, 2023 shows a clear spike in the Bronx and Queens, matching the timing of the Canadian wildfire smoke. This indicates that PM2.5 trends are not driven solely by local traffic and that extreme external events can strongly affect air quality.

Figure 6. PM and traffic distribution across time.

These bar charts compare median PM2.5 and traffic volume before COVID, during COVID, and in recovery years. Traffic decreased drastically during COVID, but PM2.5 did not improve in the same way and even increased afterward in some boroughs. This demonstrates that lowering traffic alone is not enough to significantly reduce PM2.5, and other environmental or external influences play major roles.

Figure 7. PM2.5 and traffic volume by year (line chart).

This line chart shows the movement of PM2.5 and traffic from 2016 to 2025. Traffic dropped clearly during the COVID period, but PM2.5 did not follow the same downward pattern. The 2023 wildfire spike appears independently from traffic activity, showing little long-term correlation between the two metrics. Traffic changes do not closely match PM2.5 changes.

Figure 8. Half-year PM2.5 averages (2016–2025).

Most half-year periods show stable PM2.5 levels, but the first half of 2023 exhibits a sharp and unusual rise. This spike aligns directly with the 2023 Canadian wildfires, showing how a short-term but intense event can drastically worsen air quality, even when traffic levels remain relatively unchanged.

Figure 9. Traffic volume vs PM2.5: almost no linear relationship.

In the scatter plot, each dot represents one day of traffic and PM2.5 measurements. The wide, scattered distribution shows no strong linear pattern. High traffic does not always lead to high PM2.5, nor does low traffic guarantee cleaner air. This confirms our main finding: traffic impacts PM2.5, but it is not the dominant factor behind major changes, which are mostly influenced by external environmental events.
