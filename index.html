A data-driven story about air quality, traffic behavior, and wildfire impacts.

What Makes PM2.5 So Dangerous?

PM2.5 consists of microscopic particles that enter deep lung tissue and even the bloodstream, increasing the risk of major health issues such as heart attacks, stroke, and lung cancer.

Smaller particles can bypass the body's natural defenses, reaching lower lung regions and even entering the bloodstream.

Traffic Conditions in NYC

Figure 5. Heavy traffic in Manhattan.

NYC traffic is dense throughout the day, but the data shows that high congestion does not always correspond to high PM2.5 levels.

Traffic is often blamed as the primary contributor to NYC's pollution. To test this assumption, we compare PM2.5 and traffic trends across COVID and recovery.

COVID-19 & Mobility Changes

Figure 6. COVID-19 pandemic representation.

Lockdowns drastically altered mobility patterns across NYC during 2020–2021.

Figure 7. Median PM2.5 & traffic volume across eras.

Surprisingly, traffic volume increased in several boroughs during COVID, while PM2.5 did not consistently improve. This indicates that traffic alone does not determine PM2.5 levels.

Wildfire Smoke Impact

Figure 8-a. Canada wildfire event.

Large-scale wildfires sent smoke across the East Coast, affecting cities far beyond the fire zone.

Figure 8-b. NYC sky turning orange from smoke.

Manhattan experienced severe visibility loss and hazardous PM2.5 levels during early June 2023.

Figure 9. NYC PM2.5 before vs during the 2023 wildfire.

PM2.5 jumped from a long-term average of ~6.8 µg/m³ to over 27 µg/m³ during the wildfire period. This demonstrates how extreme external events can overwhelm local emission sources like traffic.

Relationship Between PM2.5 & Traffic

Figure 10. PM2.5 and traffic volume trends (2016–2025).

These line charts show PM2.5 and traffic volume trends from 2016 to 2025 for each borough. The colored background bands mark the three eras: pre-COVID, COVID, and recovery. Traffic volume fluctuates year-to-year without a consistent long-term pattern, while PM2.5 levels shift independently and exhibit a pronounced spike in 2023. Notably, this spike occurs without any significant increase in traffic, reinforcing that the wildfire smoke event—not local vehicle activity—was the dominant driver. Overall, the two curves rarely move together, illustrating the weak correlation between the two variables.

Figure 11. Daily PM2.5 vs traffic (scatter with trend line).

Each point in this scatter plot represents one day of data. The points form a wide, diffuse cloud rather than a narrow diagonal trend, showing that busy traffic days do not reliably correspond to higher PM2.5. Light-traffic days also do not guarantee better air quality. The regression line is almost flat, indicating virtually no linear relationship between the two variables. This figure provides the most direct visual confirmation of our central finding: traffic volume alone is not a reliable predictor of PM2.5 levels in NYC. Instead, seasonal heating, temperature inversions, long-range pollution transport, and wildfire smoke explain much of the observed PM2.5 variation.

Conclusion

The data clearly shows that NYC PM2.5 is shaped far more by external factors such as wildfire smoke, seasonal heating emissions, and regional pollution transport than by traffic alone.
