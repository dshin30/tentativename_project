NYC Borough Overview

Figure 1. Four NYC boroughs analyzed in this project.

Our analysis focuses on four boroughs ‚Äî Bronx, Brooklyn, Manhattan, and Queens. Staten Island was excluded due to insufficient PM2.5 and traffic data coverage during the study period. Understanding the spatial distribution of these four boroughs helps interpret borough-level patterns in both PM2.5 trends and traffic behavior.

What Makes PM2.5 So Dangerous?

PM2.5 consists of microscopic particles that enter deep lung tissue and even the bloodstream, increasing the risk of major health issues such as heart attacks, stroke, and lung cancer.

Figure 2. PM10, PM2.5, and PM1 penetration depths.

PM10, PM2.5, and PM1 differ in how deeply they penetrate the human respiratory system: PM10 particles are generally blocked in the upper airways, PM2.5 particles can reach the deeper lung regions including the alveoli, and PM1 particles are small enough to potentially cross into the bloodstream, posing significant health risks.

Figure 3. Half-year PM2.5 averages (2016‚Äì2025).

PM2.5 usually follows a seasonal cycle, but the first half of 2023 breaks the pattern with an unusually high value, corresponding to wildfire smoke transport.

Figure 4. Annual PM2.5 averages by borough (2016‚Äì2025).

This heatmap shows moderate fluctuations from 2016‚Äì2022, followed by a dramatic spike in 2023. The spike aligns with the Canadian wildfire event, illustrating the influence of external pollution transport.

Traffic Conditions in NYC

Figure 5. Heavy traffic in Manhattan.

NYC traffic is dense throughout the day, but the data shows that high congestion does not always correspond to high PM2.5 levels. Traffic is often blamed as the primary contributor to NYC's pollution. To test this assumption, we compare PM2.5 and traffic trends across COVID and recovery.

COVID-19 & Mobility Changes

Figure 6. COVID-19 pandemic representation.

Lockdowns drastically changed how people moved through the city: subway rider decreased, remote work became widespread, and street activity declined sharply. At the same time, car usage and delivery traffic increased, creating mobility patterns unlike any other period in recent history.

Figure 7. Median PM2.5 & traffic volume across eras.

Surprisingly, median daily traffic volume increased in several boroughs during COVID, while PM2.5 did not consistently improve. This indicates that traffic alone does not determine PM2.5 levels.

Wildfire Smoke Impact

Figure 8-a. Canada wildfire event.

Large-scale wildfires sent smoke across the East Coast, affecting cities far beyond the fire zone.

Figure 8-b. NYC sky turning orange from smoke.

Manhattan experienced severe visibility loss and hazardous PM2.5 levels during early June 2023.

Figure 9. NYC PM2.5 before vs during the 2023 wildfire.

PM2.5 jumped from the 2016‚Äì2023 May average (~6.8 ¬µg/m¬≥) to over 27 ¬µg/m¬≥ during the wildfire period. At this concentration‚Äîmore than five times the WHO guideline‚Äîstudies show a 20‚Äì40% increase in asthma attacks, a 5‚Äì15% rise in heart-attack and stroke‚Äìrelated emergency visits, and measurable reductions in lung function. Even short-term exposure at this level raises long-term risks such as cardiovascular disease and cancer, and wildfire-derived PM2.5 is known to be more toxic than typical urban emissions. This demonstrates how extreme external events can overwhelm local emission sources like traffic.

Relationship Between PM2.5 & Traffic

Figure 10. PM2.5 and traffic volume trends (2016‚Äì2025).

These line charts show PM2.5 and traffic volume trends from 2016 to 2025 for each borough. The colored background bands mark the three eras: pre-COVID, COVID, and recovery. Traffic volume fluctuates year-to-year without a consistent long-term pattern, while PM2.5 levels shift independently and exhibit a pronounced spike in 2023. Notably, this spike occurs without any significant increase in traffic, reinforcing that the wildfire smoke event‚Äînot local vehicle activity‚Äîwas the dominant driver. Overall, the two curves rarely move together, illustrating the weak correlation between the two variables.

Figure 11. Daily PM2.5 vs traffic (scatter with trend line).

Each point in this scatter plot represents one day of data. The points form a wide, diffuse cloud rather than a narrow diagonal trend, showing that busy traffic days do not reliably correspond to higher PM2.5. Light-traffic days also do not guarantee better air quality. The regression line is almost flat, indicating virtually no linear relationship between the two variables. This figure provides the most direct visual confirmation of our central finding: traffic volume alone is not a reliable predictor of PM2.5 levels in NYC. Instead, seasonal heating, temperature inversions, long-range pollution transport, and wildfire smoke explain much of the observed PM2.5 variation.

Conclusion & Outlook

Our original question was straightforward: does NYC traffic determine PM2.5? After examining nine years of borough-level data, our answer is ‚Äúnot really.‚Äù

The 2023 Canadian wildfires produced the clearest and strongest PM2.5 spike in the entire dataset, overwhelming the influence of local traffic. This suggests that improving NYC air quality requires strategies addressing regional pollution transport, climate-driven wildfire risk, and building-related emissions.

After seeing that traffic and PM2.5 had almost no clear relationship, we also considered that this pattern may be partly due to the gradual increase in electric and other environment-friendly vehicles over the years. Cleaner engines and stronger emission controls can allow traffic volumes to grow without causing a matching rise in PM2.5.

Future work could combine mobility data with weather and satellite-based smoke indicators to build predictive models. Even with limitations, the project shows that ‚Äúmore traffic‚Äù is a poor shortcut for ‚Äúworse air‚Äù in NYC.
