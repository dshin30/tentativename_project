NYC PM2.5 & Traffic Volume Analysis (2016–2025)

Understanding air quality trends, wildfire impact, and the real role of traffic.

What is PM2.5 & Why Does It Matter?

PM2.5 is a fine particulate pollutant small enough to penetrate deep into the lungs and bloodstream. It is strongly linked to asthma, cardiovascular disease, and premature death. Many people believe that traffic is the main cause of PM2.5—but global studies show traffic contributes only about 27%. This study asks: Does New York City's traffic actually explain changes in PM2.5?

NYC Boroughs Analyzed

We analyze Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, and the Bronx using traffic and PM2.5 data from 2016–2025. Staten Island was excluded due to incomplete traffic coverage.

The 2023 Wildfire Event

In June 2023, smoke from Canadian wildfires reached NYC, driving PM2.5 levels above 117 µg/m³ — the worst in recorded city history. This extreme spike dominates the air-quality timeline and is unrelated to local traffic emissions.

Key Findings

  • Traffic volume shows almost no correlation with PM2.5.
  • Wildfire smoke and regional pollution transport dominate PM spikes.
  • COVID traffic reduction did not reduce PM2.5 levels.
  • Air-quality policy must address cross-border and wildfire-driven pollution.
